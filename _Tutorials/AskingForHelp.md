# How to properly ask for support

We know it is frustrating! Troubleshooting what is going wrong with your datalogger or prototype can take ages and is always difficult. But you might be in luck as your Data Foundry instance could have someone responsible for support! However to make it manageable for them to support your entire institution, we want to give you a few tips on how to ask for help. This way we can help you more effectively and understand your needs better! Following these tips will make sure you have everything ready to ask for support.

## Step 1. Summarize

Before you go into the nitty gritty of your project, it is important for us to know what you are trying to achieve on a higher level. We might know of easier, simpler or quicker ways to achieve your goal. Important here is that your goal is never getting your project or a specific component to work. Think of goals "Measuring Temperature of someone's arm", "Creating a digital twin of my bike", "Printing out this message when someone presses that button". **Tell us your goal!**

## Step 2. Draw it out

Especially when your project consists out of multiple parts, devices or services, it is important for us to understand how everything is connected to each other. Places where parts, devices or services connect to each other are often weak links, so knowing where these links are, makes it easier for us to figure out what might have gone wrong. If you **draw out all components of your system** we can not only help you spot weak links, but also help you find easier solutions or workarounds.

## Step 3. What is going wrong

In case you are working with code, it is always important to **show us what is going wrong**. For this it is important that you share all error messages or troubleshooting information with us. To do so you can for example use console messages or use commands like serial.print() the serial bus when using a microcontroller. By inserting these messages you can gain a better understanding of what might be going wrong, and gives us a clear scope of the issue. Bringing us a broken prototype without knowing what might be wrong is **not an excuse**. 

{: .warning}
Especially when using AI for coding, we want you to pay attention to the code and try to understand what might be going wrong with the generated code. If you do not have a clue what the AI generated code is doing, we can't help you...

## Step 4. Provide sufficient information

Once we know what might be wrong, for us to help it is nice to see where something might be going wrong. So when sending us error messages, make sure to also provide the code that is generating this error messages. Additional information like this allows us to understand the context of the error better, and potentially spot errors. If you are building a circuit, show us the pin-out diagram you are following so that we know what you are working with.

## Step 5. Feedback and gratitude

As the final step, you might want to give feedback about how your journey continued (and whether everything worked). Also, you want to thank everyone who has supported you. This appreciation is not only professional behavior, you also build relationships that you can reactivate in your next project (where you might need support again).
